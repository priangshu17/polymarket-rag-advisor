POLITICAL AND ELECTION PREDICTION MARKETS GUIDE

ELECTION MARKET BASE RATES
Historical base rates for election prediction markets:

US Presidential Elections:
- Incumbents running for re-election win approximately 65-70% of the time historically
- Economic conditions (GDP growth, unemployment, inflation) strongly correlate with incumbent performance
- Approval ratings below 45% at election time strongly predict incumbent loss
- The "bread and peace" model (economic growth + wartime casualties) has strong predictive power

US Congressional Elections:
- President's party typically loses seats in midterm elections (average ~25 House seats)
- Exceptions occur when president has very high approval (post-9/11 2002) or unique circumstances
- Senate races are more unpredictable due to state-specific factors

POLLING AS AN INPUT TO PROBABILITY ESTIMATES
Key considerations when using polls:
- Polling averages outperform individual polls significantly
- Early polls (>6 months out) have very low predictive validity
- Polls within 2 weeks of election have much higher reliability
- House effects: some pollsters systematically lean one direction
- Likely voter screens matter more as election approaches
- Response rates are low (~5-10%); non-response bias can be significant

Converting polling leads to win probabilities:
- A 5-point lead with 500 respondents = roughly 70-75% win probability
- A 10-point lead with 1000 respondents = roughly 85-90% win probability
- These estimates have wide confidence intervals early in campaigns

KEY POLITICAL PREDICTION CATEGORIES ON POLYMARKET

1. ELECTIONS (Presidential, Congressional, Gubernatorial)
Key signals: polling averages, fundraising totals, approval ratings, economic indicators, incumbency
Base rate anchor: start with generic base rates, update based on specific factors

2. LEGISLATION AND POLICY
Key signals: Congressional vote counts, party whip counts, leadership statements, Senate filibuster math
Note: legislative markets are notoriously difficult; many bills trade too high near 50%
Base rate: most significant legislation fails; bills need 60 Senate votes for cloture

3. GEOPOLITICAL EVENTS
Key signals: troop movements, diplomatic communications, historical conflict patterns
Base rate: most geopolitical crises do NOT escalate to military conflict
Consider: the base rate of war between specific country pairs historically

4. CENTRAL BANK DECISIONS (Fed, ECB, BOE)
Key signals: Fed futures market (CME FedWatch), inflation data (CPI, PCE), employment data, Fed speeches
The CME FedWatch tool gives real-time probability of rate decisions based on futures pricing
Fed meetings: January, March, May, June, July, September, November, December

5. REGULATORY AND LEGAL MARKETS
Key signals: court filings, legal precedent, expert legal opinions, appellate court composition
Note: legal markets have wide uncertainty; courts can surprise

READING POLITICAL SIGNALS CORRECTLY

Economic Indicators Most Predictive of Elections:
1. Real disposable income growth in year of election
2. Unemployment rate trajectory (direction matters, not just level)
3. Inflation — particularly food and energy prices felt by voters
4. GDP growth in Q2 and Q3 of election year (Abramowitz "Time for Change" model)

Warning Signs That Markets Are Mispriced:
- Market is >80% on a candidate when state-level polls show tight race
- Market hasn't updated after major news event
- Volume very low (under $50,000) suggesting price may not be reliable
- Resolution criteria are ambiguous and could lead to unexpected NO outcome

ELECTION FORECASTING MODELS AND THEIR PREDICTIONS
Several established models to reference:
- 538 model: aggregates polls with fundamental economic adjustments
- Economist model: Bayesian approach weighting polls and fundamentals
- Sabato's Crystal Ball: expert qualitative analysis
- Larry Rothenberg model: based on presidential approval and economic growth
- PredictIt and Polymarket prices themselves (wisdom of crowds)

These models should be aggregated, not treated as single sources of truth.
